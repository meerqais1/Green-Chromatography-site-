Green Analytical Chemistry Research Proposal

Title

Comparative Evaluation of Green Chromatographic Techniques in HPLC and GLC for Regulatory Applications in the Pharmaceutical and Food Industries (2023–2025)

1. Introduction

Chromatography is essential in the pharmaceutical and food industries for qualitative and quantitative analysis. HPLC and GLC are widely used due to their precision and sensitivity, especially under global purity and contamination regulations. Green Chromatography emphasizes safer solvents, reduced waste, energy conservation, and minimal environmental impact.

2. Statement of the Problem

  • Lack of comparative research on green HPLC vs. green GLC.
  • Industry uncertainty on green methods' analytical rigor.
  • Strict standards from FDA, EMA, EFSA without clear green method guidance.
  • Need for data-driven recommendations.

3. Rationale

  • Advance sustainable science and green chemistry.
  • Provide insights to regulatory agencies.
  • Modernize testing protocols.
  • Promote environmental awareness among professionals.

4. Objectives

Main Objective: Compare green HPLC and GLC in pharma and food industry applications.

Specific Objectives:

  • Identify green chromatographic practices.
  • Examine regulatory standards.
  • Assess resolution, sensitivity, cost, and environmental impact.
  • Analyze case studies of green transition.

5. Research Questions

  • What are the developments in green chromatography (2023–2025)?
  • Comparison of green HPLC vs. GLC in accuracy, cost-efficiency, and environmental impact?
  • Which regulatory agencies support green chromatography?
  • What barriers and benefits exist in green chromatography adoption?

6. Literature Review

  • HPLC for thermally unstable compounds; GLC for volatile substances.
  • Green chromatography uses renewable solvents, smaller volumes, and portable devices (e.g., SFC, ethanol-water HPLC, micro-GC).
  • Regulatory agencies now encourage greener methods.
  • Studies show ethanol-water solvents and micro-GC maintain quality while reducing impact.

7. Methodology

Research Design: Mixed-methods — quantitative (LOD, cost, waste) + qualitative (case studies, expert interviews).

Data Collection: Scientific articles (2020–2025), regulatory documents, whitepapers.

Tools: SWOT analysis, comparison tables, trend graphs.

8. Expected Outcomes

  • Full evaluation of green chromatographic applicability.
  • Guidance for industry professionals.
  • Support for sustainable regulatory updates.

9. Limitations

  • Access to industrial data is limited.
  • Green technologies are still evolving and unstandardized.
  • Regional differences in regulatory acceptance.
